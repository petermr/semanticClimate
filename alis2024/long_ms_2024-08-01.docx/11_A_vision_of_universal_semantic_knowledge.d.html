Abstract

#semanticClimate is a program of internships and volunteers working as an online and real-life team to create semantic resources for global problems, especially climate.  Using the philosophy of the late Shri SR Ranganathan combined with principles of Open Notebook Science (ONS) and Free/Open Source Software (F/OSS) we have created:

  • A toolkit for converting legacy documents to semantic form with annotations
  • A query/analysis system AMI, including programs pygetpapers, docanalysis, amilib and amiclimate. Programs are created through test-driven-development (TDD) based on legacy conversion tasks. where possible methods are generalized, abstracted and moved to libraries (amilib)

  • Semantic versions of United Nations documents
  •  the UN IPCC AR6 reports (6x chapters)
  • the UN IPCC Glossary
  • The UNFCCC (COP) reports

We work as an online team, with frequent sessions on Zoom(™), underpinned with Slack(™) and daily commits to repositories on Github following ONS philosophy (commit immediately for the whole world to see, “no insider knowledge”). This requires extensive engineering, which is described in detail. Many tasks are “human-parallelised”, where each intern has a different AR6 chapter or glossary subset but all use the same tools and help test and develop them.

The primary recording medium is Github repositories for programs, documents, discourse.

The program is ongoing, and semantic versions of the UN material are continually posted to the world. The team acts as a mentor and have successfully helped computer-literate teenagers to join.

This article contains two case studies of our software:

  • pygetpapers and docanalysis were used to query and analyse the current Open scientific literature in bulk. A scoping review of “semantic publishing” generated 101 references in (Europe)PubMedCentral. All material was automatically downloaded as JATS XML. The study showed that there was relatively little spread of the practice of semantic publishing over more than 20 years.
  • Analysis of 75 chapters of the UN Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change. The raw material was automatically converted to semantic HTML and used to create Wikipedia-enhanced dictionaries (one per chapter). The dictionaries were used to search and index the UN IPCC corpus.

The complete package (documents, software, learning protocols) is aimed at several demographics (young people, retired people, those with English as Second Language (ESL)). It is now being trialed by a group of University of the Third Age (U3A) retirees in Cambridge, UK.

Terminology

We use AMI to represent the set of programs (pygetpapers, docanalysis, amilib, amiclimate) for download, search and analysis. The AMI mascot is a kangaroo.

Terms/keywords in this article:

  • Annotation: adding links to words or phrases
  • Bibliographic Metadata:  list of authors, journal, title, year, etc. for an article
  • Legacy: documents or software, often hand-authored which do not conform to a clear schema
  • Semantic: understandable by humans and processable by machines
  • Wikimedia: Generic Resources from Wikimedia Foundation: Wikipedia, Wikidata, Wikimedia Commons, etc.
  • IPCC:  Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change
  • UNFCCC: United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change

Introduction

Inspiration from SR Ranganathan

This article is in response to an invitation to honor the appointment of Late Shri S. R. Ranganathan1 100  years after his appointment as first Librarian to the University_of_Madras . We are inspired by his biography2 and his 5 Laws of Library Science3 . We interpret them in the light of the Semantic Web4 and principles of Openness and inclusivity.

This is not a conventional scholarly article. We believe that if SR Ranganathan were active today he would have challenged the current practices of academic scholarship, breaking down the ivory tower and communicating with everyone, promoting equity and using modern tools where they were universally available without friction. He would have transcended language and geographical barriers and seen the world as his community.

This article may break some current conventions!

In the second law [“every reader their book”] Ranganathan had an inclusive view:

“[he] named children, the physically disabled, artisans [define], newly literate adults, the intellectually disabled, working-class individuals, and individuals with niche interests” [Five_laws_of_library_science ]3

Our view is that “everyone” should be able to access globally important knowledge. As examples we include

Teenagers, farmers, local government (planners), retired people

We assume that they have internet access (however limited) and can work in local teams to help share experience and resources. Our approach is general (and we’ve applied it to viral epidemics and plant chemistry). Here we take examples from “climate change” as an existential threat that everyone needs to know about. We have created an Open toolkit that anyone with Internet access can use and develop

Our material would fill several large books, so we have devised a structure similar to Wikipedia. This article is written in HTML and has a top level narrative, including diagrams, which includes links to further resources.

To help readers to understand complex terms and concepts, we have added hyperlinks pointing to other resources (articles and especially Wikipedia5)

We are driven by two main goals:

  • To make climate knowledge accessible to as many people as possible
  • To show the semantic approach and how it works

Climate Knowledge and how semantics can help

Climate is the key subject of our present and future, but it’s vast. No one understands it fully as it requires mathematics, physics, chemistry, biology, geoscience, environment, space, psychology, economics, politics, law and much else. Many scientists believe it is already too late to avoid climate catastrophe; others disagree.

But teenagers like Greta Thunberg6 have become experts and taken the data and arguments to world leaders. Our #semanticClimate7 movement and products aim to make this as easy as possible.

Climate and United Nations (UN)

Climate is the most existential threat to humankind  The UN Secretary General, Antonio Guterres, describes the latest UN report as a “Survival Guide for Humanity”.

(https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WQCABvil_2k&ab_channel=UnitedNationsCaribbean. The current article was written during the worst heatwave in Delhi (52°C) where vulnerable people spending long periods outside could die.

 Verifiable, validatable, trustable knowledge is a key tool in helping policymakers prevent the worst (“mitigate”)  and prepare for the effects (“adaptation”)

The most trustable climate knowledge is collected by a panel of thousands of experts working with the United Nations. They produce key outputs:

  • Assessment Reports (AR) every 7 years, the last was AR6 (2020-2023)8 
  • UNFCCC Reports from the yearly COP meetings9
  • Glossary of terms10

These are complex, use difficult terminology, and are written for experts. We believe that by making them semantic, filtering paragraphs, and linking to Wikimedia, they are accessible to many more people.

Climate change knowledge (UN and EPMC)

In this article we use two sources:

  • The United Nations (IPCC8 and UNFCCC9). They are verifiable, valid, and because they have thousands of authors from around the globe we (largely) trust them. That doesn’t mean we believe everything they say is factually correct. Many believe that the IPCC has been too cautious13 and temperatures will rise beyond 3°C.
  • The Open scholarly literature in EuropePubMedCentral14 and OpenAlex15 repositories16. We have developed tools (pygetpapers17 and docanalysis18) for automatically searching, downloading, and analyzing this. (see Dictionary Searching)

A major part of this work is to create semantic versions of the United Nations documents on climate, perhaps reaching 40,000 pages. We write software specifically for UN material.

Verifiable, Valid and Trusted

“Delhi records the highest ever temperature of 52.3 degrees …”21 scream headlines. Before we believe this we need to know:

  • Verifiable: where did this information come from?
  • Valid: are the data well defined?
  • Is the data trustable?

And we need semantics to help.
The source and other metadata are important. This (and many others like it) came from Indian newspapers. That’s verifiable.

Is the data well-defined?
No. Because there are no units of measurement (should include “Celsius” or “C”). (The USA uses Fahrenheit where 52F is chilly). Semantic requires units and where possible estimates of error.

Is the data trustable? We can’t say. (But many of the newspaper articles were arguing that they weren’t).

Semantics

We are inspired by the vision of the Semantic_Web04 whose goal is “to make Internet22 data Machine-readable”23 which “ may be classified into two groups: human-readable data that is marked up24 so that it can also be read by machines (e.g. microformats25, RDFa26, HTML27), and data file28 formats intended principally for processing by machines (CSV29, RDF30, XML31, JSON32). These formats are only machine readable if the data contained within them is formally structured; exporting a CSV29 file from a badly structured spreadsheet does not meet the definition “.  Most of our output will be the former and we favour HTML where possible, especially with `class` and `role` attributes.

Semantics lets machines:

  • Present the material to human in different ways, e.g. with CSS33 Styles/Paged Media which we use for IPCC Glossary
  • Separate the material into components , and select only those that are useful for a given purpose
  • Transform a component into a different representation, possibly by adding additional information
  • Combine components into a collection or add different types
  • Add hyperlinks which let humans or machines link information together
  • Extract data for further computation.

Fig. 1- An automatic semantifier (2008) for atmospheric chemistry (Chemicaltagger)34,35. It uses Natural Language Processing36 to parse into phrases, then uses lookup dictionaries, or expert systems to interpret the terms. As an example, ethanal37 (circled)  is parsed by the OPSIN program into its chemical formula which is then immediately rendered as a structure (white rectangle). The geographical terms are looked up in a Gazetteer which can then return coordinates.

The figure 1 shows automatic analysis of atmospheric chemistry language. Later we show how this technology can be extended to climate science.

Semantic publishing

The semantic web is a ground-breaking vision and implementation from Tim Berners-Lee38 where the world’s knowledge is made open and semantic. It depends on agreed standards (e.g. XML, RDF) and if fully implemented would be a giant knowledge base owned by and for the world. Semantic publishing was seen as a growth area in 2005-2010. New types of journals, conferences, were created (see Wikipedia)5.

Tim Berners-Lee (at the London Olympics) declared “This is for everyone”

Box 3

This is for everyone #london2012 #oneweb #opening ceremony @webfoundation @w3c

Some communities have enthusiastically adopted it, especially Wikimedia. Others such as scholarly publishing have fought to suppress it by paywalling knowledge and using legacy tools like PDF and bitmap39 images of data.  

The failure to adopt semantic publishing has set back knowledge publication by two decades and opened the door for mega surveillance capitalists to scrape the current legacy web into their “AI” machines.

Our textual material is many hundred pages or more and much of our discourse is not printable (software and audio/video).

Case Study Design

We report two case studies:

  • a scoping review for “Semantic Publishing” using the Open literature
  • analysis of the UN’s IPCC AR6 corpus.

Semantic Publishing

There are remarkably few articles that discuss or use semantic publishing40. In https://github.com/petermr/semanticClimate/tree/main/alis2024/sempub we have reviewed the bioscience literature for “semantic publishing” and only found a handful. The same for “nanopublications”41 which are semantic snippets (examples can be found in https://nanopub.net/ and our treatment of AR6/SYR.

  • We use EuropePMC (EPMC) as the primary source for this scoping review. Although EPMC only formally covers bioscience, in practice many other subjects in Open Access sources, and preprints, are included.
  • The primary method is to search for “Semantic publishing” and “nanopublications” using pygetpapers. This can be filtered by date. The fulltext of all articles is automatically downloaded if available.

IPCC

The current work by interns and volunteers has now created a working “alpha” version of a complete pipeline for the UN IPCC material. The process is outlined:

  1. Semantification of 75 chapters of seven UN IPCC reports (SR15, SROCC, SRCCL, AR6 WG1/2/3 and SYR). This is largely automatic using the AMI suite but continual cleaning is required and refactoring of
  2. Each member intern selects their own chapter and:
  • Writes a summary
  • collects key words/phrases/names/abbreviations and
  • Creates a per-chapter dictionary with
  • Automatic lookup within Wikipedia
  1. The dictionaries are compared and normalised, currently into:
  • Climate specific dictionaries
  • Unusual or difficult english words

  1. The member then searches their chapter with the accumulated dictionaries effectively building a multi-disciplinary index of the chapters

The commands take seconds to execute.  

Recently a citizen group (about five) in Cambridge, UK is exploring the use of these dictionaries and technology. The University of the Third Age (U3A) (retired people interested in further study) has  an 8-lecture  Climate Change course from Dr. Alastair Breward. Each lecture has extensive slides with climate terms and links. Members of the group will each analyse a chapter using the same protocol as in #semanticClimate.

Scoping query

In climate and elsewhere we need to read literature that we know nothing about! But the AMI tools have been built to help us search and understand this literature.They also give us confidence we can offer the results to the world.

Grant and Booth (2009) define a scoping review46:

“Description. This type of review provides a preliminary assessment of the potential size and scope of available research literature. It aims to identify the nature and extent of research evidence (usually including ongoing research).”

Normally this is done manually; it can take months (Figure 2).

Fig 2. -  Flow diagram to show the manual scoping of the literature (Shweata N Hegde).

pygetpapers can automate much of this, especially the initial vocabulary extraction (Box 4).

Box 4: Automated literature search with pygetpapers17

Example from CEVOpen. Search EuropePMC14 for plants with chemicals and diseases.

“Which plants , which chemicals and which diseases are most commonly mentioned in the literature?”

We start with a query like
        pygetpapers --query “plants AND chemicals AND disease” 

which gives
        INFO: Total number of hits for the query are 26487

For initial scoping we only need a few hundred articles (saved as  fulltext.xml with a searchable HTML table of all the article metadata (authors, pages, links, journals, etc.


        pygetpapers -q “plants AND chemicals AND disease”

               —-limit 200 --output phytochem_disease --xml —-makehtml

The table from makehtml consists of rows of articles and columns of bibliographic metadata). This table can then be searched on local machines (with the benefit of avoiding surveillance and connection problems).

Semantic documents and semantic publishing

An HTML document has a rich structure consisting of nested components (elements) described by tags. The commonest are

  • head. Metadata (Data about the document; authors, titles, dates, etc.). Also see the Journal Article Tag Suite for examples in XML.
  • body. The content of the article, often highly nested (divs (sections), lists, tables, figures, and paragraphs)
  • back. references, thanks,

It is useful to know what section the information is in; for example “Zika” can be a person if in authors or a virus in a paragraph. XML Documents from EuropePMC have a rich set of tags, because the US NIH47 has invested in doing this.

Wikidata and LinkedOpenData

Wikipedia has millions of pages and many of them are data-rich, especially in infoboxes48. This has been extracted and formalized into Wikidata, a collection of over 100 million items. Besides those from infoboxes, many databases (e.g. in bioscience, cities, people, books) have been copied into Wikidata43 (Figure 3).

Fig 3. - The linked open data cloud in 2020. Each point represents a subdiscipline linked to

many others. We use these as dictionaries where possible for search and annotation.

Discussion

Semantic Publishing

The #semanticClimate project has shown that Open communal development of software and informatics can provide many of the functions that Shri SR Ranganathan would have welcomed. The aggregated Open literature can be used as a giant knowledge base and integrated with Wikidata and other ontologies. This is not only a much better way of disseminating knowledge than currently (anglophone PDF) but it’s also much cheaper.

This is already technically possible and affordable.

But the surveillance publishing industry, with the failure of values in Global Northern academia, has a stranglehold on publishing (see other articles (Becerril-Garcia) (Brembs) (Chan) in this Special Issue). This Publisher-Academic Complex sells reputation, not content and has no interest in investing in semantic publication.

There are fruitful areas outside publisher control, especially if the authors’ raw manuscripts (in Word or LaTeX) are available:

  • Reports (e..g from research institutions).
  • Theses
  • Preprints

These cover a large amount of current science and could be made semantic at an acceptable cost, producing a large knowledgebase.

We work largely within the academic and research communities and we are conscious that they do not usually reach out to Ranganathan’s, or our, demographic. Academic scholarly publication is important, not just for researchers, but setting the scene for a wider range of other publications, such as the UN reports.

Semantic Publishing and Artificial Intelligence (AI)

AI is not magic. Much of it, especially the disciplines related to documents, is Machine Learning (ML) based on semantic labeling of huge data sets. If documents are already produced in semantic form then ML becomes more precise and more efficient. Indeed one of the many benefits of the semantic web is to create an Open knowledgebase for ML. We show examples of knowledge graphs in our resources: https://semanticclimate.github.io/p/en/posts/resources/).

However we are seeing commercial publishers scraping academic output of all sorts on a huge scale without transparency.  An open semantic web should be trustable, documented, with open tools. The key question is whether we are prepared to create this ourselves because there is no transparency or trust in the current gold rush for marketable content.

Recommendations for Semantic Publishing

Much of the technology for semantic publishing already exists. The NIH / NLM has pioneered the Journal Article Tag Suite (JATS) which defines the structure and semantics of scholarly articles. It is a required Open Access format by many funders, and so all publishers can already create articles in this format.

The scientific community has created many databases with persistent identifiers for objects , and Wikidata adds identifiers for metadata. Adding these to publications is a relatively inexpensive process (much can be done by automatic lookup as we have shown). With F/OSS tools authors can add identifiers during data or manuscript production. Any costs are vastly lower than the 12,000 USD charged by Springer Nature for a single “Open Access” publication.

We make the following simple inexpensive recommendations to authors and the publishing community

  • All open access articles should be openly available in JATS format. Open tools for Word2JATS and LaTeX2JATS are inexpensive to create and should be funded.
  • Identifiers for Data and metadata objects in Wikidata should be included wherever possible. For much science (chemistry, species, genes, proteins, drugs, astronomy, and many others) this can be done automatically.

  • All preprints should be available in JATS format with Wikidata identifiers.
  • All theses should be Open and available in JATS format with Wikidata identifiers.

Details:

  • The body should use HTML structure tags (div, p, span, ul/ol, table, etc.) and hyperlinks (a[href]), with images in SVG.
  • style should use CSS and paged media (see accompanying manuscript (Worthington))

If authors use community agreed Open tools, then manuscript semantics will converge to an agreed de facto standard.

Much of the tooling already exists and this is an area where libraries can make a major contribution. With Open semantic documents the literature automatically becomes an Open knowledgebase and can be searched and manipulated with graph-based tools.

We believe this would be a fitting tribute to SR Ranganathan.
